# Set Your Price

## Tool Usage

Use `web_search_exa` to research competitor pricing, find what similar products charge, and discover pricing strategies in the user's market.

## Core Principle

**Charge something. Always.** There is a massive difference between free and $1. Dan Ariely calls it the "zero price effect" — people will line up for free brownies but the line disappears at 1 cent. If you don't charge, you can't stay alive, and you can't learn what customers value.

## Two Pricing Models

### 1. Cost-Based Pricing
- Calculate costs (hosting, time, materials, payment processing)
- Add a margin (20-50% typical)
- Best for: physical products, services with clear costs

### 2. Value-Based Pricing
- Price based on value to the customer, not your costs
- A feature might cost you nothing but be worth a lot to the customer
- Best for: software, digital products, high perceived value

## Pricing Principles

1. **Start low, raise over time.** Prices go up as products improve. Expected and healthy.
2. **Pricing is not permanent.** Just another thing to iterate on.
3. **Tiered pricing is the goal.** Economy, business, first class. Same destination, different experience.
4. **The zero price effect.** Never give your product away free as default.
5. **Free trials are table stakes.** Customers expect them — offer trials with a clear path to paid.

## How to Set Your Initial Price

Ask:
1. What are your variable costs per unit/customer?
2. What are competing solutions charging?
3. What would make this a "no-brainer" purchase?
4. What price lets you be profitable from customer #1?

Don't rely on guesses for #2 — do the research:
- **Find competitor pricing**: Search `"[competitor name] pricing"` or `"[product type] pricing plans"` to find actual pricing pages.
- **Read pricing discussions**: Search `"[product type] too expensive" OR "[product type] worth the price"` to understand customer sentiment.
- **Find benchmarks**: Search `"[industry] SaaS pricing benchmarks"` or `"how to price [product type]"` for data-driven guides.

## The Math of Financial Independence

Help the user do the math:
- How much do you need per month?
- At your price point, how many customers is that?
- At one new customer per business day (260/year), when do you hit that number?
- Example: $10/month, need $2,000/month = 200 customers = less than 1 year

## Output

1. Pricing model (cost-based, value-based, or hybrid)
2. Initial price point with rationale — backed by competitor research from Exa
3. Potential tier structure for the future
4. Number of customers needed for financial independence
5. When to revisit and raise prices
